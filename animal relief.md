Easiest to grow · Broadest application · Strongest evidence · Every medicinal animal garden should contain these
1. CAPE ALOE — Aloe ferox
Field	Detail
Status	Native · Endemic to South Africa
Parts used	Leaf gel (clear), bitter yellow sap (latex)
Animals	Dogs, cats, horses, cattle, goats, sheep, poultry, rabbits
Relieves	Wounds, burns, skin infections, hot spots, fungal conditions, ringworm, cracked hooves, constipation (latex), digestive inflammation
Preparation	External: Split leaf, apply gel directly to wound or skin. Internal: Small amount of gel mixed into feed for digestive soothing. Bitter latex: tiny doses only as purgative
Growing	Full sun · Well-drained sandy/rocky soil · Drought-resistant · All SA provinces · Frost-tolerant to −4°C · Propagate by offsets or seed
Safety	⚠ Bitter latex is a strong purgative — very small doses internally. ⚠ Latex may cause cramping in cats — use gel only for cats. ⚠ Do not apply gel to deep puncture wounds (may seal infection in)
2. STALKED BULBINE — Bulbine frutescens
Field	Detail
Status	Native · Endemic
Parts used	Leaf gel (clear and yellow layers)
Animals	All species — external use
Relieves	Wounds, cuts, abrasions, burns, insect bites and stings, rashes, cracked pads, cracked hooves, ringworm, eczema, sunburn
Preparation	Snap leaf, squeeze gel directly onto affected area. Repeat 2–3 times daily. Can be mixed with coconut oil for a salve
Growing	Full sun to part shade · Any soil · Extremely drought-resistant · Spreads rapidly as groundcover · All provinces · Frost-tolerant · Propagate by division, cuttings, or seed
Safety	✅ Exceptionally safe — no known toxicity externally. The single most accessible first-aid plant for animals in South Africa
3. AFRICAN WORMWOOD — Artemisia afra
Field	Detail
Status	Native · Widespread across South Africa
Parts used	Leaves, stems
Animals	Dogs, horses, cattle, sheep, goats, poultry, donkeys
Relieves	Internal parasites (worms), respiratory infections, coughs, nasal congestion, fever, tick/flea repellent, digestive spasm, colic
Preparation	Deworming: Dried leaves crumbled into feed (2–7 days). Respiratory: Infusion (tea) added to drinking water. Repellent: Strong infusion as wash/spray on coat. Smoke: Dried branches burned near livestock housing to repel insects
Growing	Full sun to part shade · Well-drained soil · Hardy · All provinces · Frost-tolerant · Grows to 2m · Propagate by cuttings or division
Safety	⚠ Contains thujone — do not use on pregnant animals. ⚠ Use in courses (7 days on, 14 days off), not continuously. ⚠ Moderate doses only for cats
4. WILD GARLIC / SOCIETY GARLIC — Tulbaghia violacea
Field	Detail
Status	Native · Endemic
Parts used	Leaves (primarily), bulbs
Animals	Horses, cattle, goats, sheep, poultry — external use preferred for dogs/cats
Relieves	Ticks (repellent), fleas (repellent), flies (repellent), internal parasites, respiratory congestion
Preparation	Repellent wash: Crush leaves, steep in hot water 30 min, cool, spray or sponge onto coat. Livestock deworming: Crushed leaves mixed into feed in small amounts. Poultry: Crush leaves into drinking water
Growing	Full sun to part shade · Most soils · Drought-resistant · Clump-forming · All provinces · Frost-tolerant · Propagate by division
Safety	⚠ Related to Allium family — contains sulphur compounds. ⚠ Do NOT give internally to dogs or cats (haemolytic anaemia risk). External wash is safe for dogs/cats. ✅ Safe internally for livestock and poultry in moderate amounts

9. ROSE GERANIUM — Pelargonium graveolens
Field	Detail
Status	Native · South African origin (Pelargonium genus is centred in SA)

10. ROOIBOS — Aspalathus linearis
Field	Detail
Status	Native · Endemic to Cederberg, Western Cape
Parts used	Leaves and fine stems (processed as tea)
Animals	Dogs, cats, horses, rabbits
Relieves	Skin allergies, itching, eczema, digestive soothing, antioxidant support, anxiety (mild), colic
Preparation	Skin wash: Brewed rooibos tea (cooled) applied to itchy/allergic skin — soak, don't rinse. Internal: Rooibos tea in drinking water for digestive soothing and antioxidant support. Eye wash: Weak, well-strained, cooled rooibos for minor eye irritation
Growing	Challenging outside native range — requires acidic, sandy, nutrient-poor soil. Full sun. Best grown in Western Cape or in acidic container mixes. Propagate by seed (scarify first)
Safety	✅ Exceptionally safe — caffeine-free, low tannin, hypoallergenic. Safe for all species including cats. One of the safest teas for animal use
11. PUMPKIN — Cucurbita maxima / C. moschata
Field	Detail
Status	Cultivated · Widely grown across South Africa
Parts used	Seeds (primarily), flesh
Animals	Dogs, cats, horses, poultry, goats, cattle, rabbits
Relieves	Intestinal worms (roundworm, tapeworm — cucurbitacin paralyses worms), diarrhoea, constipation (fibre), digestive regulation, nutritional supplementation
Preparation	Deworming: Raw, ground pumpkin seeds mixed into feed. Dogs: 1 tsp per 5 kg body weight daily for 5–7 days. Cats: ½ tsp daily for 5 days. Horses: 100–200g ground seeds. Poultry: Ground seeds in feed. Digestive: Cooked pumpkin flesh mixed into feed — regulates both diarrhoea and constipation
Growing	Full sun · Rich, well-watered soil · Summer crop · All provinces · Direct sow after frost · Propagate by seed
Safety	✅ Very safe — no known toxicity. Seeds and flesh safe for all listed species. One of the safest natural dewormers
12. MORINGA — Moringa oleifera
Field	Detail
Status	Cultivated · Naturalised in Limpopo, KZN, Mpumalanga
Parts used	Leaves, seeds
Animals	Dogs, horses, cattle, goats, sheep, poultry
Relieves	Nutritional deficiency, immune weakness, inflammation, poor coat/feather condition, lactation insufficiency in dairy animals, poor wound healing, antioxidant support
Preparation	Nutritional supplement: Dried, powdered leaves mixed into feed. Dogs: ½–1 tsp daily. Horses: 1–2 tbsp daily. Poultry: 5% of feed mix. Cattle/goats: 50–100g dried leaf daily. Seeds: Crushed seeds purify drinking water (flocculent)
Growing	Full sun · Well-drained soil · Frost-sensitive (Limpopo, KZN, Mpumalanga, coastal areas) · Fast-growing to 10m · Propagate by seed or cuttings · Can be coppiced for continuous leaf harvest
Safety	⚠ Root and root bark contain spirochin — toxic. Use leaves and seeds only. ✅ Leaves and seeds are safe and highly nutritious

16. DEVIL'S CLAW — Harpagophytum procumbens
Field	Detail
Status	Native · Kalahari region (Northern Cape, North West, Limpopo)
Parts used	Secondary tuber (root)
Animals	Dogs, horses (primary), cattle
Relieves	Arthritis, joint pain, hip dysplasia (symptom management), tendon/ligament inflammation, general pain, digestive stimulation, appetite loss
Preparation	Joint support: Dried, powdered root mixed into feed. Dogs: 100–400mg per 10 kg body weight daily. Horses: 10–20g dried root daily. Course of 4–8 weeks. Infusion: Decoction (simmered 15 min) added to feed or water
Growing	Deep sandy soil · Full sun · Hot, dry climate · Difficult to cultivate — slow-growing · Source sustainably (wild populations threatened) · Propagate by seed (slow germination)
Safety	⚠ Not for pregnant animals (may stimulate uterine contractions). ⚠ May potentiate blood thinners and cardiac medications. ⚠ Not for animals with gastric ulcers (stimulates acid). ✅ Well-tolerated in recommended doses — the most scientifically validated SA plant for animal joint pain

SECTION D: PREPARATION METHODS

Standardised methods for consistent, safe, effective remedies
METHOD 1: INFUSION (Tea)

For: Leaves, flowers, soft aerial parts
Process:

    Bring water to boil
    Remove from heat
    Add fresh or dried plant material (1 handful fresh OR 2 tablespoons dried per 1 litre water)
    Cover and steep 15–30 minutes
    Strain through fine cloth
    Cool to room temperature

Storage: Refrigerate — use within 48 hours
Administration: Add to drinking water, drench, or sponge onto coat
METHOD 2: DECOCTION

For: Bark, roots, tubers, woody stems
Process:

    Chop or crush plant material
    Place in pot with cold water (2 tablespoons material per 1 litre water)
    Bring to boil, then reduce to simmer
    Simmer 20–30 minutes with lid on
    Strain through fine cloth
    Cool

Storage: Refrigerate — use within 48 hours
Administration: Add to drinking water, drench
METHOD 3: POULTICE

For: Direct wound/skin/joint application
Process:

    Crush or mash fresh plant material
    Apply layer directly to affected area
    Cover with clean cloth or bandage
    Change every 12–24 hours
    Clean area between applications

Alternative: Soak cloth in strong infusion/decoction, apply as warm compress
METHOD 4: INFUSED OIL

For: Wound healing, ear drops, skin conditions, massage
Process:

    Fill clean glass jar ¾ full with dried plant material (Calendula flowers, Chamomile, etc.)
    Cover completely with carrier oil (olive oil, coconut oil, sunflower oil)
    Seal and place in sunny spot for 4–6 weeks, shaking daily
    OR: Warm gently in double boiler at 40–50°C for 4–6 hours (quicker method)
    Strain through fine cloth, squeeze out remaining oil
    Store in dark glass bottle

Storage: Cool, dark place — 6–12 months
Administration: Apply drops to ear, apply to skin, massage into coat
METHOD 5: DIRECT GEL APPLICATION

For: Aloe, Bulbine, Sour Fig — immediate first aid
Process:

    Break or cut leaf
    Squeeze or scrape gel/juice
    Apply directly to wound, burn, or skin condition
    Do not cover (let air-dry) unless bandaging is needed for protection
    Reapply 2–3 times daily

METHOD 6: DRIED HERB IN FEED

For: Deworming, immune support, anti-inflammatory, nutritional
Process:

    Harvest plant material
    Dry in shade with good airflow (not in direct sun — preserves active compounds)
    Crumble or powder when fully dry
    Mix into feed at recommended amounts

IMPLICATION FOR CONSERVATION PLANTING

When creating wildlife corridors, rehabilitation zones, or game farm habitats:

    Plant medicinal species that target animals naturally seek
    Maintain Artemisia afra stands in grassland margins — browsed by many ungulates during parasite peaks

    Maintain Aloe diversity — browse source for tortoises, ungulates, and rhino
    Leave Spekboom stands — critical browse and hydration for many species
    Allow Helichrysum grasslands — aromatic species used by birds and mammals

### ⚠ CRITICAL: WHEN TO GO TO THE VET IMMEDIATELY

**Do NOT attempt herbal treatment — seek veterinary care immediately for:**

| Emergency | Why |
|---|---|
| Snake bite | Antivenom may be required — time-critical |
| Suspected poisoning | Specific antidotes needed — bring plant sample |
| Bloat in cattle (distended left flank, distressed) | Can be fatal within hours — requires trocar or tube |
| Colic in horse (rolling, sweating, pawing) | Can indicate twisted gut — surgical emergency |
| Difficulty breathing (choking, blue gums) | Airway obstruction — immediate intervention |
| Prolapse (any organ visible outside body) | Surgical — keep moist with clean cloth, transport |
| Fracture (limb at abnormal angle) | Stabilise and transport — splint if possible |
| Heavy bleeding not stopping with pressure | May need suture or cautery |
| Seizures lasting more than 3 minutes | Neurological emergency |
| Inability to urinate for 12+ hours | Urinary blockage — fatal if untreated |
| Dystocia (difficult birth — no progress for 1+ hour) | May require caesarean or manipulation |
| Eye injury with penetration | Surgical — prevent rubbing, transport |

**Herbal medicine supports. It does not replace emergency veterinary care.**

### EMERGENCY HERBAL FIRST AID — WHAT TO DO WHILE WAITING FOR THE VET

| Situation | Immediate Herbal Action |
|---|---|
| **Bleeding wound** | Apply pressure. When bleeding slows, apply Bulbine gel (2) or Aloe gel (1) — antimicrobial, promotes clotting |
| **Burn** | Cool with water 10 min. Apply Bulbine gel (2) — thick layer. Do not cover unless necessary to prevent contamination |
| **Insect sting (swelling)** | Apply Bulbine gel (2) or Sour Fig juice (5) immediately. Monitor for anaphylaxis — if breathing difficulty, VET |
| **Snake bite (while transporting to vet)** | Keep animal calm and still. Do NOT cut, suck, or tourniquet. Sutherlandia (17) infusion can be given en route as immune support — but antivenom is the treatment |
| **Heat stress (panting, drooling, unsteady)** | Move to shade. Cool water on pads/hooves, groin, neck. Spekboom (48) crushed leaves for moisture. Small sips of Rooibos (10) cooled tea |
| **Diarrhoea (acute, profuse)** | Guava leaf decoction (46) — the fastest-acting plant astringent. Give frequently in small amounts. Ensure hydration |
| **Choking on plant material** | Do NOT push deeper. If visible, remove carefully. Vet if lodged. After clearance — Sour Fig juice (5) or Aloe gel (1) to soothe throat |
| **Eye contamination** | Flush with clean water. Then Calendula (7), Chamomile (23), or Rooibos (10) — well-strained, cooled infusion as eye wash |
| **Post-trauma shock (pale gums, rapid breathing, weakness)** | Keep warm. Elevate hindquarters slightly. Sutherlandia (17) or Rooibos (10) warm infusion — small amounts orally if conscious. VET |

## SECTION K: QUALITY CONTROL, STORAGE & SHELF LIFE

### HARVESTING QUALITY

| Principle | Practice |
|---|---|
| **Timing** | Harvest mid-morning after dew evaporates, before midday heat |
| **Plant health** | Only harvest from healthy, vigorous, pesticide-free plants |
| **Season** | Harvest leaves before flowering (highest active compound concentration). Harvest flowers at full bloom. Harvest roots/tubers during dormancy |
| **Sustainability** | Never take more than 30% of any individual plant. Rotate harvest areas. Leave some plants unharvested for seed production |
| **Cleanliness** | Wash hands. Use clean, sharp cutting tools. Avoid soil contamination on harvested material |

### DRYING

| Method | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Preferred** | Hang in bundles or spread on screens in shade with good airflow. Avoid direct sun (degrades active compounds). Avoid humidity (causes mould) |
| **Temperature** | Ideal: 25–35°C with air movement |
| **Duration** | Leaves: 3–7 days. Flowers: 5–10 days. Roots/bark: 7–14 days (slice thinly first) |
| **Test** | Material should snap/crumble when dry — not bend. No remaining moisture feel |
| **Emergency fast-dry** | Oven at lowest setting (50–60°C) with door ajar. Check frequently. Not ideal — heat degrades some compounds |

### STORAGE

| Container | Airtight glass jars (brown/amber preferred), sealed paper bags inside tins, or vacuum-sealed bags |
|---|---|
| **Environment** | Cool, dark, dry. Avoid heat, light, and humidity |
| **Labelling** | Plant name, part used, date harvested, date of expiry |

## SECTION L: INTEGRATION WITH CONVENTIONAL VETERINARY CARE

### THE INTEGRATION PRINCIPLE

> **Herbal medicine and conventional veterinary medicine are not opposites — they are complementary layers of animal care.**

| Layer | Role |
|---|---|
| **Prevention** | Herbal medicine excels — immune support, parasite management, nutritional supplementation, stress reduction |
| **Minor conditions** | Herbal medicine often sufficient — small wounds, mild digestive upset, mild skin conditions, parasite maintenance |
| **Moderate conditions** | Herbal medicine as primary with veterinary monitoring, or as adjunct to conventional treatment |
| **Severe / Emergency** | Conventional veterinary medicine primary. Herbal medicine as supportive adjunct only |
| **Chronic conditions** | Best results from integration — conventional management + herbal support (arthritis, allergies, recurring infections) |

### WHEN HERBAL MEDICINE IS THE APPROPRIATE PRIMARY APPROACH

| Situation | Example |
|---|---|
| Routine parasite prevention/management | Artemisia afra (3), Pumpkin seeds (11) — seasonal courses |
| Minor wound care | Bulbine (2), Calendula oil (7) |
| Mild digestive upset | Chamomile (23), Wild Mint (8), Pumpkin flesh (11) |
| Skin allergies (mild) | Rooibos wash (10), Calendula (7) |
| Insect repellent | Rose Geranium (9), Lippia javanica (6) |
| Stress/anxiety (mild) | Lavender (15), Chamomile (23) |
| General immune support | Moringa (12), Sutherlandia (17) |
| Nutritional supplementation | Moringa (12), Spekboom (48) |

### WHEN TO INVOLVE A VETERINARIAN

| Situation | Why |
|---|---|
| Condition not improving after 3–5 days of herbal treatment | May need diagnosis or stronger intervention |
| Animal refusing food or water for 24+ hours | Dehydration and underlying condition |
| Fever not responding to herbal treatment within 24 hours | May indicate serious infection |
| Recurrent condition despite herbal management | Needs diagnostic workup |
| Any condition worsening despite treatment | Treatment may be inappropriate or insufficient |
| Before using herbs on animals on other medications | Drug-herb interactions |
| Pregnant or lactating animals | Many herbs contraindicated |
| Very young animals (neonates) | Sensitive to many compounds |
| Any emergency (see Section J) | Time-critical intervention needed |

### HERB-DRUG INTERACTIONS TO KNOW

| Herb | Interacts With | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| Devil's Claw (16) | Blood thinners (warfarin), cardiac drugs, diabetic drugs | Potentiation |
| Turmeric (28) | Blood thinners, chemotherapy drugs | Potentiation of blood thinning |
| Ginger (29) | Blood thinners | Potentiation |

**Rule:** If an animal is on **any** chronic medication, consult a veterinarian before adding herbal treatment.
