## PREAMBLE

South Africa likely holds the highest concentration of documented antimicrobial plant species per unit land area of any nation on Earth This reflects the convergence of the Cape Floristic Region (approximately 9 600 vascular species of which a substantial proportion show pharmacological activity) with the extraordinarily diverse Savanna Grassland Forest and Thicket biomes of the north and east Together these biomes contain well over 300 plant species with peer-reviewed antimicrobial documentation spanning gram-positive gram-negative acid-fast mycobacterial and fungal organisms including drug-resistant strains such as MRSA and MDR Mycobacterium tuberculosis

This reference covers all verified and credibly reported antimicrobial species across all eight primary South African biomes organised by growth form Trees · Shrubs · Herbs and Geophytes · Succulents · Grasses and Sedges · Climbers · Aquatic and Riparian Each entry includes accepted taxonomy common names biome distribution provincial range active parts optimal harvest season primary active compound classes target organisms potency class and mechanism category Western Cape cultivation notes are integrated throughout

Data priorities: South African university pharmacological research (Universities of Pretoria Johannesburg KwaZulu-Natal Stellenbosch and the Medical Research Council) and peer-reviewed ethnobotanical and bioassay literature All MIC potency classes reflect dilution assay data unless otherwise indicated

### Detailed Profiles — Priority Tree Species

---

WARBURGIA SALUTARIS (Pepperbark Tree) [!E] — IUCN Endangered

This species is likely the single most potent antimicrobial tree in the South African flora exhibiting inhibitory activity at very low concentrations across a broad pathogen spectrum The active compounds are sesquiterpene dialdehydes of which polygodial is the most studied and most potent Polygodial operates through multiple mechanisms simultaneously: disruption of the plasma membrane through reaction with amino groups on membrane phospholipids inhibition of mitochondrial electron transport and modulation of H-ATPase activity The compound is markedly more active against gram-positive organisms and fungi than gram-negatives with documented MIC values of 0.02 to 0.05 mg/mL against S aureus and values as low as 0.003 mg/mL against sensitive Candida strains Activity against drug-resistant MTB has been demonstrated in vitro placing this species among the most pharmacologically significant trees on the continent

Critical conservation note: This species is IUCN Endangered due to wild bark stripping for traditional medicine trade and should under no circumstances be collected from wild populations Cultivation from seed is possible (slow germination; 3-4 weeks at 25C) and forest-garden cultivation is both achievable and strongly encouraged This species merits the highest conservation-cultivation priority in any ethnobotanical garden planning

---

TERMINALIA SERICEA (Silver Terminalia)

This widely studied savanna tree delivers a complex of triterpenoid acids and tannins with documented activity against MRSA E coli and Klebsiella pneumoniae at high potency Bark aqueous extracts achieve MIC values of 0.19 to 0.78 mg/mL against S aureus depending on extract preparation The compound sericoside (a triterpenoid glycoside unique to this genus) contributes significantly to gram-negative activity The bark is the primary active organ with greatest compound concentration occurring during the dry season when water-stress-induced secondary metabolite accumulation peaks The species has a wide distribution across Savanna and Grassland biomes and is moderate to fast-growing from seed making it a viable candidate for savanna garden plantings in the summer-rainfall region

---

EUCLEA UNDULATA and EUCLEA CRISPA (Guarri Species)

The guarri species in family Ebenaceae are notable for naphthoquinone chemistry particularly 7-methyljuglone which exhibits MIC values of approximately 1.3 ug/mL against Mycobacterium tuberculosis — placing it among the most potent anti-TB compounds found in any SA plant genus Naphthoquinones operate through redox cycling generating reactive oxygen species that damage bacterial DNA and disrupt electron transport simultaneously Euclea crispa is the more adaptable species occurring across fynbos margins Nama-Karoo Savanna Grassland and Albany Thicket biomes and is well-suited to cultivation in the WC under low-water conditions Both species are drought-tolerant slow-growing shrubs or small trees suitable for boundary planting

---

COMBRETUM MOLLE (Velvet Bushwillow)

Among the Combretum genus this species has the most comprehensively documented antibacterial profile combining tannin-mediated protein binding with triterpenoid membrane disruption achieving H-class potency against both gram-positive and gram-negative organisms including several MRSA strains The bark also contains combretol a compound with specific membrane-active properties Bark harvest should follow the dry season The species adapts well to a wide range of soil types and shows moderate growth rate making it a strong candidate for savanna-climate garden planting in the summer-rainfall interior regions

---

CRYPTOCARYA LATIFOLIA (Broad-leaved Cryptocarya)

This Southern Cape and KZN forest tree is one of the pharmacologically most significant discoveries in recent SA plant research The bark contains cryptofolione a gamma-butenolide compound with exceptionally potent activity against Mycobacterium tuberculosis at MIC values of approximately 3 to 6 ug/mL for isolated compound — placing it in VH class for TB activity The compound appears to operate through inhibition of mycobacterial protein synthesis mechanisms supplemented by membrane disruption This species deserves considerably more research attention than it has received and is amenable to cultivation in forest garden settings in the WC and EC

## SECTION 2: SHRUBS

The South African shrub layer contains some of the pharmacologically best-characterised antimicrobial species in the entire African flora Pelargonium sidoides roots have generated an internationally registered pharmaceutical product (EPs 7630 / Umckaloabo) and the Agathosma (buchu) genus has been commercially traded for over two centuries The Helichrysum genus contributes arzanol a phloroglucinol compound found nowhere else in nature Fynbos-endemic shrubs are particularly concentrated with antimicrobial secondary metabolites likely reflecting adaptation to the fire-driven regeneration and pathogen-rich wet-winter climate of the Cape

### Detailed Profiles — Priority Shrub Species

---

PELARGONIUM SIDOIDES (African Geranium / Umckaloabo)

This species has generated the single most commercially successful antimicrobial pharmaceutical derived from South African flora The standardised root extract EPs 7630 (marketed globally as Umckaloabo) is approved in Germany and used across Europe for acute bronchitis and upper respiratory infections with multiple randomised clinical trials supporting efficacy The active chemistry centres on coumarins particularly umckalin (7-hydroxy-5,6-dimethoxycoumarin) and related compounds supplemented by gallic acid and proanthocyanidin complexes The primary mechanism appears to be inhibition of bacterial adhesion to respiratory epithelial cells rather than direct bactericidal action — a uniquely important mechanism among SA antimicrobial plants This is supplemented by immunomodulatory effects including natural killer cell activation and interferon-gamma induction Activity against Mycobacterium tuberculosis at MIC values of 0.015 to 0.03 mg/mL for the coumarin fraction has been documented in vitro

The species occurs across the Eastern Cape in rocky dry slopes extending into the Western Cape and Northern Cape where it favours well-drained stony soils Roots should be harvested from 3 to 4 year old plants in late autumn to winter when carbohydrate and secondary metabolite accumulation in the storage root is at maximum The species is straightforward to cultivate in WC garden conditions preferring well-drained slightly alkaline rocky substrate and full to partial sun

AGATHOSMA BETULINA and AGATHOSMA CRENULATA (Buchu)

The buchu species are endemic to the Western Cape Fynbos biome and represent the most commercially developed South African antimicrobial plant group outside of the Aloe genus Historically used by Khoisan communities for millennia they entered global commerce via Dutch VOC traders in the 17th century The primary antimicrobial activity derives from the essential oil of which diosphenol (dominant in A betulina) and pulegone with isomenthone (dominant in A crenulata) are the principal compounds The essential oil disrupts bacterial cell membranes through direct lipid bilayer interaction with documented activity particularly against uropathogenic E coli and Staphylococcus saprophyticus — making buchu preparations specifically relevant for urinary tract infections Both species require well-drained acidic fynbos-type soils at pH 4.5 to 5.5 with good sun exposure They are available from WC specialist nurseries and legal to cultivate Wild collection is now subject to regulatory restrictions

---

LIPPIA JAVANICA (Fever Tea / Umsuzwane)

This aromatic Verbenaceae shrub is one of the most widely traded and used antimicrobial herbs in southern African traditional medicine particularly in KwaZulu-Natal The essential oil is particularly rich in thymol and carvacrol — well-characterised membrane-active monoterpenoids with broad-spectrum bactericidal activity at low concentrations Both compounds disrupt the bacterial cell membrane by incorporating into the lipid bilayer increasing membrane permeability and collapsing the proton motive force a mechanism studied in detail across multiple laboratory groups Documented activity extends to MRSA Klebsiella pneumoniae and Haemophilus influenzae at H-class potency and the plant is one of the most consistently active against gram-negative species among all SA shrubs The plant is widespread across Savanna and Grassland biomes and can be cultivated in WC with mild frost protection Leaf harvest peaks in summer when heat drives essential oil accumulation

---

MYROTHAMNUS FLABELLIFOLIUS (Resurrection Plant)

This extraordinary species belongs to the monogeneric South African family Myrothamnaceae and demonstrates one of the most unusual biochemical profiles of any SA shrub Upon near-complete desiccation (up to 98 percent water loss) the plant appears completely dead but resumes full metabolic activity within 24 hours of rehydration During the dry-dormant state secondary metabolite concentration increases markedly particularly the 3,4,5-tri-O-galloylquinic acid and ellagitannin compounds responsible for the strongest antimicrobial activity MIC values of 0.13 to 0.25 mg/mL against S aureus and comparable activity against E coli place this in the High potency class Arbutin content may contribute indirect antimicrobial activity through conversion to hydroquinone This plant is found on dry rocky outcrops across NC EC FS LP and MP biomes and is best harvested in the fully dry state when compound concentration is highest

---

HELICHRYSUM ODORATISSIMUM (Imphepho)

This traditional incense plant from Zulu and Xhosa medicine communities contains arzanol — a phloroglucinol compound of unique chemical structure found in no other plant genus on Earth International research groups have documented dual inhibition of HIV-1 integrase and 5-lipoxygenase by arzanol making this one of the most pharmacologically multi-targeted compounds from southern African flora Arzanol and related phloroglucinols exhibit bacteriostatic activity against S aureus and strong anti-biofilm activity against Candida The traditional use involves burning dried aerial parts as incense with the smoke functioning as an antimicrobial delivery mechanism — a form of fumigation-based environmental disinfection This practice has received increasing scientific attention as a potentially effective method for reducing airborne and surface pathogen loads

---

ARTEMISIA AFRA (African Wormwood / Wildeals / Lengana)

This species is the most widely used medicinal herb in South Africa by both number of users and geographic range occurring naturally across all nine provinces The antimicrobial essential oil contains camphor 1,8-cineole thujone and artemisia ketone achieving H-class potency against gram-positive organisms and moderate activity against gram-negatives Documented activity against Haemophilus influenzae and Mycobacterium smegmatis (as an MTB surrogate) is notable The primary mechanism involves membrane disruption through terpenoid-lipid interaction supplemented by enzyme inhibition The plant is extremely easy to cultivate in WC gardens requiring minimal water once established and self-seeds prolifically It may be the single best entry-point herb for any WC antimicrobial garden for its combination of potency accessibility and zero maintenance requirements

### Detailed Profiles — Priority Herbaceous Species

---

ALOE FEROX (Bitter Aloe)

Aloe ferox is South Africa's most commercially important aloe species producing both the bitter crystallised latex sap (aloe crystals used medicinally and exported globally) and the clear inner leaf gel The antimicrobial activity resides primarily in the bitter yellow latex layer located between the green outer rind and the clear inner gel This layer contains anthraquinones including aloin A aloin B (barbaloin) aloe-emodin emodin and chrysophanol operating primarily through two mechanisms: redox cycling to generate reactive oxygen species that damage bacterial DNA and direct membrane disruption through anthraquinone insertion into lipid bilayers Documented MIC values against S aureus range from 0.33 to 0.78 mg/mL for crude latex preparations with isolated aloe-emodin achieving H-class potency Activity against Helicobacter pylori at MIC values below 0.1 mg/mL for isolated anthraquinone fractions is of clinical relevance given global gastric ulcer disease burden Aloe ferox grows throughout the Western Cape Albany Thicket and Succulent Karoo biomes and is extremely easy to cultivate from offsets or seed requiring minimal water once established

---

TULBAGHIA VIOLACEA (Society Garlic)

This South African Amaryllidaceae native contains allyl sulfide compounds directly analogous to those of true garlic (Allium sativum) including dimethyl disulfide dimethyl trisulfide and S-alk(en)ylcysteine sulfoxides that generate marasmin upon tissue disruption The antimicrobial mechanism is functionally identical to garlic allicin: thiosulfinate and disulfide compounds inhibit thiol-containing bacterial enzymes (cysteine proteases thioredoxin reductase) and disrupt membrane integrity through sulfur-containing lipid interactions Documented MIC values against S aureus in the H-class range (0.1 to 0.4 mg/mL from fresh bulb preparations) with activity extending to MTB Candida and Streptococcus This species is among the most adaptable plants for WC garden conditions tolerating full sun to partial shade requiring only moderate water and suffering no significant pests It grows as a dense clump with continuous leaf harvest possible year-round and is available from virtually every nursery in South Africa at low cost making it arguably the single highest value-to-accessibility antimicrobial herb for the WC grower

---

ARCTOPUS ECHINATUS (Porcupine Thorn / Platdoring)

This prostrate spiny fynbos specialist is endemic to the Cape Floristic Region with distribution extending from the Western Cape coast through the Overberg and into the Eastern Cape The deep taproot contains falcarindiol a C17 polyacetylene compound also found in other Apiaceae genera with documented moderate to high antimicrobial activity against S aureus E coli and Candida Falcarindiol likely operates through alkylation of sulfhydryl groups in bacterial enzymes and direct membrane interaction The isofuranocoumarins provide supplementary activity potentially through DNA interaction The species grows in seasonally dry fynbos typically in sandy or stony well-drained soils and is rarely cultivated commercially but can be established from fresh seed in appropriate fynbos substrate

CARPOBROTUS EDULIS (Sour Fig)

The sour fig is ubiquitous along the Western Cape coastline and has well-documented antimicrobial activity particularly relevant for oral and dermal applications The leaf juice contains a complex of flavonoids (quercetin kaempferol myricetin) and condensed tannins with documented activity against oral pathogens including Streptococcus mutans and Candida albicans at M to H potency in biofilm inhibition assays Traditional use includes application of freshly squeezed leaf juice to wounds burns and skin infections and use of fruit juice as throat gargle The flavonoid mechanism involves inhibition of glucosyltransferase enzymes (key for biofilm formation) and direct membrane disruption in exposed pathogens The plant is effectively available in unlimited quantities along the WC coastline and has no conservation concern unlike many SA medicinal plants

EUPHORBIA SPECIES — SAFETY CRITICAL ANNOTATION

The two euphorbia species listed achieve High-class antimicrobial potency through phorbol ester and terpenoid compounds however these same compounds are severe tissue irritants and tumour-promoting agents The milky latex causes intense chemical burns on skin and permanent blindness if contacted with the eyes These species are listed here for completeness of the antimicrobial flora record only and should not be used for any self-treatment purpose

CYMBOPOGON VALIDUS (Giant Turpentine Grass / Isiduli)

This native South African Cymbopogon species is distinguished from pantropical lemongrass (C citratus) and citronella grass (C nardus) by its piperitone-dominant essential oil rather than citral or citronellal Piperitone is a bicyclic monoterpenoid that achieves strong membrane disruption activity with documented H-class potency against S aureus E coli and Candida albicans The compound is also active against respiratory pathogens The species grows in moist grassland and savanna particularly in KZN and EC at moderate altitude reaching significant height (to 2 metres) making it visually distinctive Essential oil yield and piperitone content peak in late summer and early autumn as plants approach reproductive maturity Harvest involves cutting aerial parts at 20 to 30 cm from ground level with vigorous regrowth from rhizomes Steam distillation yields 0.5 to 1.5 percent essential oil by fresh weight

CISSAMPELOS CAPENSIS (Dawidjieswortel)

This climbing species from the Menispermaceae is endemic to the Cape Floristic Region extending into adjacent dry biomes of the Northern Cape and Eastern Cape The roots are highly prized in Cape Malay and Khoisan traditional medicine for treatment of chest and respiratory infections stomach complaints and inflammatory conditions The active compounds are bisbenzylisoquinoline alkaloids including hayatine cissampeline and structurally related compounds analogous to tetrandrine and berbamine well-known from other Menispermaceae genera internationally These alkaloids demonstrate H-class activity against MRSA and documented in vitro activity against Mycobacterium tuberculosis likely through multiple simultaneous mechanisms including DNA intercalation enzyme inhibition and membrane disruption The root architecture concentrates alkaloids making winter-spring harvest of established plants optimal This species can be cultivated in WC gardens in sheltered positions with partial shade and adequate moisture and is occasionally available from specialist fynbos nurseries

## SECTION 11: WESTERN CAPE CULTIVATION GUIDE

The Western Cape Mediterranean climate (wet cool winters; hot dry summers) is perfectly matched to a wide range of South African antimicrobial species particularly fynbos endemics and savanna species with dry-season metabolite peaks The following cultivation notes are stratified by biome-type garden design enabling any WC grower to establish a multi-layered antimicrobial garden covering all seasons and a broad pathogen spectrum

### Cultivation Zone A — True Fynbos Garden
pH 4.5 to 5.5 · Sandy or rocky well-drained substrate · Full sun · Minimal irrigation in summer · Winter-rainfall adapted

AGATHOSMA BETULINA and A CRENULATA (Buchu) — Establish from nursery-grown stock in autumn in well-drained sandy-acid soil in full sun Water minimally in summer (natural fynbos regime) Do not fertilise with phosphorus-rich fertilisers as this inhibits fynbos root associations Harvest leaves from year two onwards picking the oil-rich branchlets of leaf tips in spring and summer Dry rapidly at below 40C to preserve diosphenol content

ERIOCEPHALUS AFRICANUS (Wild Rosemary) — One of the easiest fynbos shrubs to establish Highly drought-tolerant once established Cuttings root readily in autumn Cut aerial parts in spring before full flowering when alpha-terpineol content is highest Dry-hang in ventilated shade

SALVIA AFRICANA-LUTEA and S AFRICANA-CAERULEA (Beach and Blue Sage) — Both establish readily from cuttings in autumn or spring S africana-lutea is more coastal and extremely wind-tolerant S africana-caerulea prefers slightly more moisture and part-shade inland Both can be harvested year-round but are most concentrated in spring and early summer

ASPALATHUS LINEARIS (Rooibos) — Grows well in well-drained sandy acid soil Only viable from fresh seed (germination requires scarification by nicking the seed coat or brief hot water soak) Inoculation with appropriate Rhizobium is strongly recommended for nitrogen fixation Harvest young green shoots in late summer Cut bundles of fine-needled shoots sun-wilt briefly then bruise and allow enzymatic fermentation (2-24 hours until orange-red colour develops) then dry for traditional preparation Unfermented (green rooibos) preserves maximum aspalathin content for antimicrobial activity

ARCTOPUS ECHINATUS (Porcupine Thorn) — Low prostrate spine-armed rosette Grows from deep taproot in sandy fynbos soils Establish from fresh seed in autumn Root activity peaks from year 3 onwards when taproot diameter becomes substantial Do not attempt to transplant established plants as the taproot severs and plants die Handle carefully as spines are painful

CLIFFORTIA RUSCIFOLIA — Very easy from seed or cuttings in fynbos-type soils Grows vigorously on rocky slopes Can be used as a low hedge or boundary plant Leaves and stems harvested year-round

OSMITOPSIS ASTERISCOIDES (Belskruie) — Grow in moist but well-drained positions within the fynbos garden Harvest aerial parts in flower — strong camphor aroma

---

### Cultivation Zone B — Cape Transition Shrubland Garden
pH 5.5 to 7.0 · Mixed loam · Full to part sun · Moderate water · Wide climatic tolerance

ARTEMISIA AFRA (Wild Wormwood / Wildeals) — This is the single most recommended species for any South African medicinal garden It is essentially indestructible once established tolerating drought light frost poor soil salt wind and neglect Grow from cuttings (root in 3-4 weeks under mist) or fresh seed Plant 1 metre apart as it spreads vigorously Harvest young grey-green aerial parts before or at early flowering when camphor and cineole content are highest Air-dry in shade bundles Prepare as tea (steep covered — do not boil) or steam inhalation or topical decoction

DODONAEA VISCOSA (Sand Olive) — Grows in virtually any well-drained substrate across SA Tolerates coastal salt wind and moderate frost Fast-growing to 3-4 metres Harvest mature leaves year-round with peak active compound in summer Exceptionally hardy pioneer species that establishes quickly and provides wind shelter for more sensitive species

TARCHONANTHUS CAMPHORATUS (Wild Camphor Bush) — Most adaptable species in this reference — occurs in all 9 SA provinces Tolerates extreme drought wind salt frost and poor soil Fast-growing aromatic shrub to 5 metres Harvest spring and summer leaves Steam-distillation yields a high-camphor essential oil Excellent windbreak or hedgerow species that combines practicality with pharmacological value

LEONOTIS LEONURUS (Wild Dagga) — Pioneer herb-shrub reaching 2 metres Establish from seed or cuttings in well-drained mixed loam Reseeds prolifically Harvest orange flowering aerial parts in late summer Regarded as the most accessible combined antimicrobial and immunostimulant species in the transition zone

HALLERIA LUCIDA (Tree Fuchsia) — Fastest-growing WC forest edge shrub-tree to 10 metres with bird-attractive tubular flowers Tolerates poor soil and moderate drought Establishes easily from cuttings Plant in partial shade or forest-edge conditions Bark and leaves available year-round

RHOICISSUS TOMENTOSA (Wild Grape) — Establish on a fence or trellis from cuttings Grows vigorously in part-shade or full sun with moderate moisture A 2-metre trellis plant can provide continuous leaf harvest from spring through autumn and the woody roots develop significant resveratrol content from year 4 onwards

---

### Cultivation Zone C — Succulent and Karoo Garden
pH 6.0 to 8.0 · Sandy gritty low-organic substrate · Full sun · Minimal irrigation · Exceptional drought tolerance

ALOE FEROX (Bitter Aloe) — Easily established from seed or offsets in any well-drained substrate Grows to 3 metres Leaf sap (bitter yellow latex) accessible from year 3 onwards by severing lower mature leaves at the base Collect the yellow sap that drains from the cut surface (not the clear inner gel) into a clean container Allow to crystallise or use fresh First harvest from a mature plant yields 50 to 100mL of liquid bitter sap per leaf Replant offsets that form at the base annually

BULBINE FRUTESCENS (Stalked Bulbine) — Arguably the simplest antimicrobial ground-cover in the SA flora Propagates by division or cuttings and constantly self-propagates Hardy to drought frost salt and neglect Harvest leaf gel by splitting a mature leaf Press gel directly onto wounds burns or infected skin as a topical antimicrobial Harvest year-round

CARPOBROTUS EDULIS (Sour Fig) — Grown as a succulent ground-cover on any dry slope Will root from any cutting pressed into sandy soil Tolerates coastal exposure completely Leaf juice expressed from a mature leaf by hand-squeezing is immediately available year-round for topical wound treatment oral rinse or throat gargle

PORTULACARIA AFRA (Spekboom) — Extremely easy from cuttings which root on contact with soil Tolerates any well-drained substrate drought and light frost Edible and antimicrobial leaves available year-round from established plants Also the most efficient carbon-sequestering species per hectare in the South African flora

---

### Cultivation Zone D — Forest Garden and Shaded Conditions
pH 5.5 to 7.0 · Rich organic mulched soil · Part to full shade · Regular moisture · Wind-sheltered

CURTISIA DENTATA (Assegai) — Afrotemperate forest tree to 10 metres Grows slowly from seed (sow fresh) in shade Establish as a forest canopy species in a sheltered garden Bark and leaves available from established cultivated specimens

RAPANEA MELANOPHLOEOS (Cape Beech / Kaapse Boekenhout) — Most common canopy tree of the WC Afrotemperate forest zone Establishes from fresh seed planted in shaded moist conditions Growth is moderate Extremely important keystone tree for forest garden establishment as it provides canopy for shade-sensitive understorey species

CRYPTOCARYA LATIFOLIA (Broad-leaved Cryptocarya) — Grow in deep fertile forest soil with consistent moisture in a shaded position Slow-growing but pharmacologically outstanding — the MTB activity of cryptofolione places this species in a world-class category for cultivation in forest gardens

TULBAGHIA VIOLACEA (Society Garlic) — Thrives in shade or partial shade in forest-garden settings as well as full sun Easy division propagation Clumps double annually Year-round leaf harvest Probably the highest cumulative annual antimicrobial yield per unit of garden space of any SA species given its ease perpetual growth and H-class activity

## SECTION 12: PREPARATION METHODS AND STABILITY

Antimicrobial compounds from South African plants are extractable by several methods each with different efficiency profiles for the compound classes present A mismatch between extraction method and target compound class significantly reduces activity

### Method Reference Table

| Method | Best Compound Classes | SA Species Well-Suited | Stability of Preparation |
|--------|----------------------|----------------------|--------------------------|
| Fresh leaf juice (no solvent) | Allyl sulfides; gel-phase anthranols; succulent flavonoids | Tulbaghia violacea · Bulbine frutescens · Carpobrotus edulis | Use within hours; volatile and unstable |
| Cold-water infusion (covered; no boiling) | Volatile essential oil terpenes; labile alkaloids; some coumarins | Artemisia afra · Lippia javanica · Agathosma spp. | Use within 24 hours; refrigerate |
| Hot-water decoction (simmered 20-30 min) | Tannins; triterpenoids; stable flavonoids; saponins | Terminalia sericea bark · Vachellia spp. bark · Syzygium · Aloe ferox | 48-72 hours refrigerated |
| Ethanol extract (50-70% ethanol; 7-14 day maceration) | Broad-spectrum; coumarins alkaloids resins lipophilic diterpenes | Pelargonium sidoides · Cissampelos capensis · Euclea spp. | 6-12 months at room temperature |
| Vinegar extraction (apple cider vinegar; 4 weeks) | Alkaloids (protonation enhances dissolution); some flavonoids | Artemisia afra · Leonotis leonurus | 6+ months; palatable |
| Honey maceration | Stable flavonoids phenolics; good vehicle for wound application | Helichrysum · Aspalathus linearis · Tulbaghia | 12+ months; synergistic with honey's own activity |
| Vegetable oil infusion (warm; 2-4 weeks) | Lipophilic essential oil terpenes; fat-soluble diterpenes | Eriocephalus africanus · Salvia spp. · Artemisia afra | 3-6 months in dark; topical use |
| Steam distillation (essential oil) | Volatile monoterpenes and sesquiterpenes | Cymbopogon spp. · Eriocephalus · Lippia javanica · Agathosma · Heteropyxis | 12-24 months sealed dark cold |
| Dried powder (fine-milled dry material) | Stable tannins; stable flavonoids; some anthraquinones | Aloe ferox (bitter crystals) · Terminalia bark · Vachellia pods | 12+ months sealed desiccated dark |

Critical Stability Notes:

Allyl sulfide compounds (Tulbaghia species) degrade rapidly on heating — use fresh or very lightly warmed preparations only and never boil
Sesquiterpene dialdehyde compounds (Warburgia salutaris polygodial) are relatively heat-stable and can be prepared as hot decoctions without major loss of activity
Essential oil terpenes are fully volatile at boiling temperature — always prepare essential-oil-rich herbs as covered cold or warm infusions never open rolling boils
Anthraquinone compounds (Aloe species Bulbine species) are stable to heat but degrade over extended storage if exposed to oxygen and light — amber glass sealed storage is mandatory
Tannin preparations (all bark decoctions) are significantly reduced in activity by milk proteins — do not combine bark decoctions with dairy

## SECTION 14: SYNERGY AND COMBINATION PRINCIPLES

The antimicrobial chemistry of the South African flora lends itself exceptionally well to synergistic combination — a principle deeply embedded in southern African traditional medicine practice where multi-plant preparations are the norm rather than the exception

TANNIN PLUS ESSENTIAL OIL COMBINATIONS — Tannin-rich species (Vachellia spp. Terminalia sericea) combined with essential-oil-rich species (Artemisia afra · Lippia javanica · Eriocephalus africanus) produce documented synergistic effects against both gram-positive and gram-negative organisms The mechanism reflects simultaneous outer membrane destabilisation by tannin-protein binding (increasing essential oil penetration) and essential oil-mediated membrane disruption

ANTHRAQUINONE PLUS TANNIN COMBINATIONS — Aloe ferox or Bulbine species combined with bark decoctions (Terminalia sericea · Syzygium cordatum) produces enhanced anti-biofilm activity beyond either component alone This combination is standard in many KZN traditional preparations

COUMARIN PLUS ANTHRAQUINONE COMBINATIONS — Pelargonium sidoides root combined with Aloe ferox sap is an historically documented Cape Malay-tradition preparation with the anti-adhesion mechanism of umckalin complementing the bactericidal anthraquinone activity

ALLYL SULFIDE PLUS ANY COMBINATION — Tulbaghia violacea leaf or bulb juice appears synergistic with essentially all other compound classes due to the thiol-reactive mechanism operating through entirely different biochemical pathways than tannin or terpenoid compounds This species is an ideal universal addition to any multi-component preparation

NAPHTHOQUINONE PLUS DITERPENE COMBINATIONS — Euclea species naphthoquinone bark combined with Croton sylvaticus or Leonotis leonurus diterpene material produces documented anti-MTB synergy of particular relevance in the South African TB disease context
